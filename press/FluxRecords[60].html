VII

Once upon a time all music was made by hand. It was crafted with skills acquired over many years of practice. It served the purpose of a refined environment to the very few and very rich. It favoured quiet conversation over sublime subjects: Are Time And Space The Conditions For Human Experience? or, Is The Experience Of Time Merely The Effect Of Gravity? or, Why Are We Having Such A Hard Time To Find A Place To Park Around Here?

These times are referred to as the time of salon music. By the time they reached the United States things changed or, shall we say, got contemporary. In May 1865 -- 100 years before I was born in Germany -- American-born Louis Gottschalk was mentioned in a San Francisco newspaper as having "travelled 95,000 miles by rail and given 1,000 concerts". However, he was forced to leave the United States later that year because of a scandalous affair with a student at the Oakland Female Seminary in, of all places, Oakland, California. He never returned to the United States.

Admittedly, I also had an affair with a student of a Young Ladies' Seminary in Oakland, California, albeit 131 years later. But as hard as I looked, these really are the only similarities between me and the beloved 19th century composer of salon music. I never left the United States and married my sweetheart.

We are also rich, we sit in a salon at the Paris Casino in Las Vegas, we discuss the third of the aforementioned sublime subjects but we only end up at TGI Fridays on a Friday. Luckily for us and all other sophisticated Situationists of the 21st century, I have just the right even-handed environment to our one-hand-always-on-the-smart-phone times.

These are my Jetudes 18, 19 & 20. Studies for the Solo Piano, made up on the spot by hand, then edited to perfection and finally served here as a smooth operator’s sonata. Go ahead, have a quiet conversation if any of this has anything to do with what the word Jazz originally stood for.
